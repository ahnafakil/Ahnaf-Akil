This is the post I've been writing in my head for about a year.

I'm Ahnaf — IT Support Technician by day, learner by night, and as of this morning the owner of a tiny corner of the internet at ahnafakil.com. I built this site to do three things: have a real portfolio, force myself to actually finish something, and be honest, in public, about a goal I'm working toward.

The goal is software engineering. I'm not there yet. This is the beginning of the path, not the end.

## Where I am right now

My day job is IT Support Technician in Troy, Michigan. I help users when their accounts get locked, when Outlook stops syncing, when Active Directory groups don't propagate, when the printer gives up on life. It's good work. It's taught me more about how software actually behaves in the real world than any tutorial ever could.

Working in IT, you see a particular pattern over and over: the same five problems come in every week, on different people, forever. Account lockouts. Mailbox permission cleanups. New-hire access requests that should have been automated three years ago. Each one takes ten or fifteen minutes individually, but they pile up.

The first time I wrote a small script to handle one of those recurring tickets, something clicked. I felt — for the first time at work — like I'd actually *moved* something. Not patched it. Not worked around it. Changed the shape of the problem.

That's when I started thinking seriously about software.

## What I'm learning

Right now, the thing I'm learning is the web.

That might sound modest. People in tech sometimes talk about HTML and CSS and JavaScript like they're the kiddie pool — something to wade through on the way to the deep end. I don't think that's right. The web is where most software is delivered now. Building a real, polished, deployed web project from scratch — domain and DNS, design system and accessibility, content and deployment — teaches you more about software than just memorizing syntax in the abstract.

So that's what I'm doing. This site is the proof of work. It's vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No frameworks, no shortcuts. I designed the visual system around five shades of slate, built it up component by component, set up a content management system that lets me write blog posts like this one, and shipped the whole thing on a custom domain.

It is not the world's most sophisticated codebase. But every line of it I understand. That matters more to me right now than building something flashy with tools I'm not yet ready for.

## Where I'm going

The long-term goal is software engineering. Not a side project. Not a hobby. A career.

I'm being deliberate about how I get there:

- **Steady job, steady learning.** I'm not quitting IT to "go find myself" in a bootcamp. I'm using the stability of my current role to fund and sustain the learning underneath it.
- **Finish things.** I'd rather ship a small, polished project every couple of months than start ten projects I never complete.
- **Build the foundation first.** HTML, CSS, JS, then a real backend language, then frameworks. I want to actually understand what I'm writing before I import it.
- **Write about it.** Including the parts that aren't going well. Especially those parts.

I have no idea exactly how long this takes. Could be a year. Could be three. I'm OK with that, as long as the work is real and I'm getting a little better every month.

## Why I'm doing this in public

Two reasons. First, public commitments are harder to back away from. Saying "I'm going to become a software engineer" out loud, on a website with my name on it, makes the goal real in a way that private daydreaming never did.

Second, there are a lot of people on this path — IT folks, career-switchers, people who didn't take a traditional CS route — and most of the writing about it either makes it sound trivially easy (it isn't) or impossibly hard (also not true). I'd rather add an honest voice to that conversation than another marketing one.

If you're walking a similar road, the contact form on this site works. So does my email. Comparing notes is one of the most valuable things you can do when you're learning anything.

## What's coming next

I'll be writing here regularly — not on a strict schedule, but consistently — about three things:

1. **What I'm learning.** Specific, technical posts on whatever I'm working through.
2. **What carries over from IT.** The unexpected ways operations experience shows up when you sit down to write code.
3. **What I'm building.** Project write-ups, post-mortems, and the occasional "this didn't work" piece.

The first version of this post was written at midnight on a Friday after a long day of tickets. That feels right. The work that matters most usually gets made in the margins, until one day you decide to call it the main thing.

Today is that day. I'm calling it the main thing. The path starts here.

— *Ahnaf*
